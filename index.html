Freedom of Expression

by Solomon Shalom Lijo

Some of the clearest moments of expression in my life have been quiet ones. They've happened late at night, sitting in front of a screen, rewriting the same few lines of code—not because they were broken, but because something about them didn't feel right. I've learned to trust that feeling. It's usually my reminder that what I'm building belongs to a collective experience and carries consequences beyond me.

I express myself most honestly through building. When I code or design systems, I'm not just solving problems; I'm thinking about people—who will use this, who might struggle with it, and who might be left out entirely. Each project becomes a small act of translation, turning care into structure and intention into design. That instinct is rooted in a principle I've carried with me for years: to love your neighbor as yourself—not as an abstract idea, but as a daily responsibility and a form of stewardship over the systems we share.

Reflection is the other place my voice shows up. I notice how environments shape behavior—how a classroom feels safer when someone listens, or how a single word can invite participation or quietly shut it down. These observations stay with me, guiding how I lead, collaborate, and take on the mantle of responsibility when building with and for others.

Freedom of expression, to me, isn't about being the loudest voice in the room. It's about having the freedom to pause, to revise, and to grow without losing honesty. It's about choosing responsibility over convenience and curiosity over certainty, especially when what we build affects more than ourselves.

I'm drawn to communities that understand expression this way—not as performance, but as presence. Places where individuality is trusted and the collective good is taken seriously. That's where I feel most myself: building carefully, listening closely, and carrying the mantle of stewardship with humility and care.
